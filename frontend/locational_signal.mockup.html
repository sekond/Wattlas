2 · Redispatch vs negative prices
by month
Stormy, high-wind months bring both the most redispatch — power physically re-routed because the corridor is full — and the most negative prices. Yet every region pays the same single wholesale price, so neither signals where the grid is short or long.

3 · The split that was decided against
the contested economics, as a range
On 15 December 2025 Germany retained the single DE-LU bidding zone (the Aktionsplan Gebotszone). The economics of splitting it are genuinely contested — so Wattlas presents them as a range, with both poles cited, and computes no split price of its own.

Decision (Dec 2025): one zone retained — Wattlas computes no split price
Germany–Luxembourg stays a single bidding zone: one wholesale price, even when the grid can't move power from the windy north to the industrial south. The case for splitting is that smaller zones would signal scarcity and pull investment where it's needed — ENTSO-E's bidding-zone review (the DE5 configuration) estimated roughly −€613m less redispatch and about +€339m in welfare from a split, though on 2019-vintage data. The case against is that the gains are small and the disruption large — several academic studies find north–south price spreads of under €3/MWh, while one zone preserves price solidarity between regions, avoids structurally higher southern prices, and keeps the market liquid. The decision went to retaining one zone. Wattlas takes no side and computes no split price — it shows the physical mismatch the debate is about.
