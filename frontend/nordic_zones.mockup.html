The Nordics already split the price zone Germany debates
Sweden, Norway, Denmark and Finland run not one price but twelve — a cheap, hydro-rich north and a demand-heavy, continent-connected south. Here's what living with split zones looks like, and what it teaches the German single-zone question.

2 · How far prices diverge
Sweden's far-north SE1 and far-south SE4 are one country, two prices. They track together in mild months and split hard in winter, when the south leans on expensive imports while the north still has hydro — the localised signal a single zone would average away.

3 · The lesson for Germany
Germany has the same physical split — windy north, demand-heavy south — but clears at one price and manages the gap with curtailment and redispatch. The Nordics priced the split instead.
Germany · one zone
One price nationwide; the north–south gap shows up as curtailment + redispatch, not in the price.
Nordics · twelve zones
Prices diverge by region, signalling where the grid is tight and where to build.
The Nordics are split into many price zones, so a windy, hydro-rich north can clear cheaper than a demand-heavy south — the prices reflect where the grid is constrained. This is the live version of the split Germany debates. Supporters say zonal prices steer investment where it's needed; critics say they push structurally higher prices onto some regions and add complexity. Wattlas takes no side — it shows what splitting looks like in a system that already did it.
