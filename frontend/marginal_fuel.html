Why the price is what it is: the fuel on the margin

The single most expensive power plant needed to keep the lights on sets the wholesale price for everyone. That plant is the "marginal fuel" — and it is why a war thousands of kilometres away can show up on a German electricity bill.

What "marginal fuel" means

Wholesale electricity clears as a single price per hour. Generators are stacked cheapest-first into a "merit order": wind and solar bid near zero because their fuel is free, then nuclear, then coal, then gas. The grid dispatches up that stack until supply meets demand — and the price everyone is paid is set by the last, most expensive plant that had to run.

That last plant is the marginal one. When renewables alone cover demand, the marginal plant is cheap and prices fall — sometimes below zero. When demand outruns the cheap stack, gas is usually the marginal fuel, and the price tracks the cost of gas plus the CO2 permit the plant must buy to burn it. This is the geopolitics-as-price-driver story: a gas-supply shock moves the marginal cost, and the whole market follows.

Why Wattlas doesn't compute it (yet)
Scope boundary — a deliberate choice, not an omission

Wattlas does not attribute prices to a marginal fuel. Doing so honestly requires a merit-order model plus a live feed of gas (TTF) and CO2 (EU ETS) prices to know where each fuel sits in the stack. No free, licence-clean source for those is currently wired into the pipeline.

We could infer a marginal fuel from generation mix and price alone — but that would be a model, not a measurement. It would carry assumptions about plant efficiencies, fuel costs, and bidding behaviour that we cannot validate against an authoritative source. Presenting a modelled attribution next to Wattlas's measured ENTSO-E data would blur the line between what we observe and what we guess.

So we hold this boundary on purpose. If a free, licence-clean TTF + CO2 feed becomes available and the methodology can be stated and validated, marginal-fuel attribution is a natural next slice. Until then, we explain the mechanism rather than fabricate the number.

What we show instead

The pieces the marginal-fuel story is built from are already measured and on the site — drawn straight from ENTSO-E, no modelling required:

  • The Mix — actual generation by fuel type, hour by hour. This is the raw material of the merit order: you can see when renewables are covering demand and when fossil plant has to step in.
  • Carbon — production-based carbon intensity (gCO2/kWh), shown on the dashboard. A close proxy for how fossil-heavy the marginal supply is at any moment.
  • Pulse — the daily price rhythm. The shape of this curve is the marginal fuel changing through the day, even though we don't name the fuel.

Together these show the effect of the marginal fuel — the mix shifting and the price moving with it — without claiming a precise attribution we can't yet measure.
