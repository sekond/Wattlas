France's nuclear: where it sits, who it powers, when it dips
France runs on a handful of big nuclear sites instead of scattered renewables. Here's where that power is made, which régions lean on it, and what happens to the whole country when the fleet goes down for maintenance — or a heatwave warms the rivers.

1 · The fleet — where the power is made
nuclear sites + host départements
A handful of large sites on the Loire, Rhône, the eastern rivers and the north coast power the entire country — the centralised opposite of Germany's scattered wind and solar. Red circles are demand centres; note how much Île-de-France consumes with almost no local generation.

2 · Who exports, who imports
régional net balance
Generation minus consumption by région. Nuclear-dense régions (Centre-Val de Loire, Grand Est, Normandie) run big surpluses; demand-heavy Île-de-France and generation-poor PACA and Bretagne lean on the rest of the country.

Régional net balance: exporters Centre-Val de Loire, Grand Est, Normandie; importer Île-de-France.
France is one bidding zone: one wholesale price nationwide. A région's surplus or deficit is a physical balance (generation − consumption), not a regional price — the same reason Germany's north–south split doesn't show in prices. Illustrative placeholder.
3 · When the fleet dips — and what fills the gap
monthly output by source
Nuclear isn't "always on". Output falls in spring and summer for refuelling and maintenance, and heatwaves force reactors down when rivers run too warm to cool them. Hydro, gas and imports fill the gap.
