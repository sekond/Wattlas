Britain's wind is in the north — and it's paid to switch off
Scotland makes far more clean power than it can use; when the grid south of it is full, wind farms are paid to stop. Here's how clean each region is, what the bottleneck costs, and how Britain's fix compares with Germany's and the Nordics'.

2 · Scottish wind, paid to stop
When the grid can't carry Scottish wind south (the B6 boundary), the operator pays wind farms to turn down — and other plants to turn up. The "constraint" bill spikes in windy winter months. It's the British equivalent of Germany's redispatch, with a transparent price.

A constraint payment is a managed grid-stability cost — wind paid to reduce, not energy discarded by choice — a real, published cost consumers ultimately pay. Source: NESO / Elexon. Figures illustrative.

Germany, the Nordics and Britain all face renewable-rich regions a congested grid can't fully serve. Germany keeps one price and curtails + redispatches; the Nordics split into price zones; Britain keeps one price and pays constraint payments. Each has trade-offs — Wattlas shows the mechanism, not a verdict.
