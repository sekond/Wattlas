European electricity, explained

Eight ways to read Europe's power markets — what a day costs, what generates it, how countries pull apart, how clean it is, and how it shifts over years. Pick a zone and a window; every panel that can, follows along. Open data from ENTSO-E and the German TSOs, pre-computed and static.

Spread — the daily gap

How wide is the gap each day between the cheapest and most expensive hour — and how often prices go negative. The swing batteries and demand-shifting live on.

Daily TB1 = max − min hourly price; red bars had negative-price hours, amber is the widest day. Source: ENTSO-E.

What actually generates the electricity, fuel by fuel. France leans on flat nuclear; Germany rides volatile wind and solar with gas and coal filling the gaps.

Stacked average generation by fuel, GW. Gaps render as breaks, never fabricated zeros. Source: ENTSO-E.

Mismatch — residual load

The demand left for conventional plants and batteries after wind and solar have done their bit. It dips midday and peaks in the evening — which is why prices peak then too.

Residual load = demand − wind − solar (GW); the shaded band is met by wind+solar. Can go negative. DE-LU, whole period. Source: ENTSO-E.

How far neighbouring zones' prices drift — and why. When an interconnector fills up, the cheaper side can't rescue the dearer one and prices decouple.

Carbon — how clean each hour is

Grid carbon intensity against renewable share: intensity falls as wind and solar rise, and France's nuclear reads far cleaner than coal-heavy hours.

Brown = carbon intensity (left), green = renewable % (right). Production-based, IPCC AR5 lifecycle factors.

Curtailment — wasted clean power

Full view ↗

Wind and solar thrown away when the grid can't absorb or move it — the cost of Germany's north–south bottleneck. It spikes on stormy, negative-price days.

Green = curtailed renewable energy (MWh/day); red = negative-price hours that day. Germany. Source: netztransparenz.de.
